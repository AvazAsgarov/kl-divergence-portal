Question 1

What is KL Divergence and what does it measure?

Click to see answer

Answer:

KL Divergence (relative entropy) is a statistical measure of the difference between two probability distributions, P and Q. From an information theory perspective, it measures the average information loss (in bits or nats) when representing the true distribution P using an approximate distribution Q.

Question 2

Why is KL Divergence not considered a mathematical distance metric?

Click to see answer

Answer:

Because KL Divergence is asymmetric: DKL(P || Q) ≠ DKL(Q || P). A mathematical distance metric must be symmetric (distance from A to B equals B to A) and satisfy the triangle inequality, both of which KL Divergence violates.

Question 3

What does the asymmetry of KL Divergence represent in practice?

Click to see answer

Answer:

It means the information loss going from P to Q is different than from Q to P. For example, Forward KL DKL(P || Q) forces the model to cover the entire support of P (mean-seeking), while Reverse KL DKL(Q || P) causes the model to focus only on major modes of P (mode-seeking).

Question 4

What are the minimum and maximum possible values of KL Divergence?

Click to see answer

Answer:

The minimum value is 0, which occurs if and only if P = Q (distributions are identical). The maximum value is +∞. If the true distribution has P(x) > 0 but the model predicts Q(x) = 0, the divergence becomes infinite because the model claims a real event is impossible.

Question 5

What is the "zero probability" problem and how is it resolved?

Click to see answer

Answer:

If the model predicts Q(x) = 0 for an event that can occur (P(x) > 0), the log term causes division by zero, driving KL to infinity. This is resolved using Laplace Smoothing (adding a small epsilon to all probabilities) or clipping probability values to a tiny non-zero limit (e.g., 1e-15).

Question 6

Where is KL Divergence used in Machine Learning?

Click to see answer

Answer:

1. Data Drift Detection: Measuring if production data distribution has drifted from training baseline.
2. Variational Autoencoders (VAE): Regularizing the latent space distribution toward a standard normal distribution.
3. T-SNE: Matching probability distributions in high and low dimensional spaces.
